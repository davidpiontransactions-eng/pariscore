Abstract

The main objective of this research is to develop a predictive model for determining the outcomes of
mixed martial arts (MMA) fights using statistical analysis. The model incorporates both traditional
first level variable and newer, constructed variables that offer a unique perspective on the sport.
Logistic regression is employed to predict wins and losses based on these variables, and the model's
predictive ability is then compared to that of baseline models. To achieve this goal, data on MMA
fights is collected and preprocessed using statistical software, including SPSS. Various statistical
techniques are utilized for feature engineering and selection to identify the most important variables
that impact the outcome of fights. The chosen variables are then used to develop the logistic regression
model, which is fine-tuned to optimize its predictive ability. The model is found to have a significantly
higher predictive ability than the baseline models, as confirmed by cross-validation. The constructed
variables provide a unique perspective on the sport and offer insights into the factors that contribute to
success in MMA.

1 Introduction

In recent years, statistical analysis in sports has become an important area of research for both
academics and professionals. Statistical methods are widely used in various sports to improve
performance, strategy, and player evaluation. For instance, in baseball, Sabermetrics involves detailed
statistical analysis to measure player performance and make strategic decisions (Lewis, 2003). In
basketball, advanced metrics such as Player Efficiency Rating (PER) and Win Shares help in assessing
a player's overall contribution to the team (Hollinger, 2002). Similarly, in soccer, data analysis tools
like Expected Goals (xG) and player tracking systems are utilized to evaluate team performance and
tactics (Anderson and Sally, 2013).

However, mixed martial arts (MMA) have received little attention in terms of statistical analysis.
While MMA has grown in popularity over the years, especially with the establishment of regulated
leagues like the Ultimate Fighting Championship (UFC), the use of advanced statistical methods in
this sport is still in its nascent stages. This presents an opportunity for further research and application
of statistical techniques to enhance understanding and performance in MMA.

Despite the tremendous growth of MMA as a sport and the large fan base it has garnered, there is a
shortage of statistical models to predict the outcomes of fights. This lack of statistical analysis has left
a gap in the ability to understand matchups between fighters and predict winners. Predicting outcomes
is not only valuable for analyzing the sport but is also of significant interest for betting purposes.
Therefore, this study aims to develop novel statistical models to predict the outcomes of MMA fights.
We will develop models based on new statistical methods and compare them with more basic models
that rely on winning percentages.

To achieve this objective, we will collect and preprocess data on MMA fights, and then engage in
feature engineering and selection to identify the most relevant variables. We will use regression
models with carefully chosen parameters to predict the outcomes of fights and evaluate the
performance of these models using metrics such as the Brier score and rescaled R-squared (Barnett,
2019). The models will be validated using cross-validation. The overall analysis process will involve a
careful selection of statistical techniques to ensure the models are statistically sound.

1.1 Background and Context of MMA as a Sport

Mixed Martial Arts (MMA) is a dynamic full-contact combat sport that has rapidly gained popularity
over the past few decades, with millions of fans tuning in to watch events such as the Ultimate
Fighting Championship (UFC). MMA allows the use of both striking and grappling techniques, both
standing or on the ground, from a wide range of martial arts and combat sports. The origins of MMA
can be traced back to the early 1900s when various forms of full-contact fighting were practiced in
different parts of the world. However, it was the creation of the UFC in 1993 that brought MMA into

the mainstream in North America. During the early days of the UFC, there were no rules or
regulations, and fights descended into brutal brawls, drawing criticism from politicians and the media
(Hoffman, 2020). To make the sport safer and more appealing to a wider audience, the UFC
implemented a set of rules in 2000. Today, MMA is a highly regulated sport, and strict rules govern
everything from the size of the fighting area to the types of strikes that are allowed. Fighters compete
in different weight classes, and they are subjected to rigorous medical testing before and after fights to
ensure their safety. Although MMA has faced criticism for its violent nature, proponents of the sport
argue that it is no more dangerous than other combat sports, such as boxing, and that the strict
regulations in place make it as safe as possible for athletes.

MMA has also been credited with breaking down barriers between different martial arts styles, as
fighters from various backgrounds compete against each other. This has led to the development of new
training methods and techniques as fighters seek to gain an edge over their opponents. Overall, MMA
is a sport that continues to evolve and grow, with millions of fans around the world tuning in to watch
the action (Mendelson, 2016). While it has faced criticism in the past, its strict regulations and
growing popularity suggest that it will continue to be a major force in the world of combat sports for
years to come (Dyer et al., 2016).

1.2 Rules and Judging

MMA judging is a process of evaluating and scoring fights to determine the winner. The scoring
system used is called the 10-point must system, where each round is scored separately by three judges.
In the UFC, fights are typically divided into three rounds for non-title fights and five rounds for
championship bouts. Each round is assessed based on four key factors: effective striking, grappling,
octagon control, and effective aggression (Weber and Fromm, 2012).

    ŌĆó Effective striking refers to clean and impactful strikes that land on the opponent, such as
         punches, kicks, knees, and elbows. Judges consider the quality, quantity, impact, and accuracy
         of these strikes when scoring.

    ŌĆó Grappling involves techniques like takedowns, submission attempts, and ground control.
         Judges evaluate how effective a fighter's grappling techniques are, including their ability to
         execute takedowns, reverse positions, and attempt submissions. Maintaining control on the
         ground and advancing position can also earn points.

    ŌĆó Octagon control is about assessing which fighter is dictating the pace and location of the fight.
         The fighter who controls the center of the octagon and is more active in engaging their
         opponent usually receives points in this category.

    ŌĆó Effective aggression refers to a fighter who consistently moves forward, presses the action,
         and actively seeks to engage their opponent. This can include aggressive striking, grappling,
         and attempts to finish the fight.

score of 10-8, indicating a significant advantage. In rare cases where a round is overwhelmingly one-

In a three-round fight, the judges' scores from each round are added up to determine the winner. If a
fight goes to a decision and ends in a draw, an additional sudden victory round may be added to
determine the final winner.

2.1 Overview of previous studies on statistical analysis in MMA

In the field of mixed martial arts (MMA), there have been several studies conducted on statistical
analysis, which have examined various factors that influence the outcomes of fights. Petr Yanovich
conducted one of the earliest studies in 2010, which analyzed the relationship between fighters'
physical characteristics and fight outcomes. Yanovich found that physical characteristics, such as
height and reach, were not significant predictors of fight outcomes, but age and experience were
significant predictors (Yanovich, 2010).

In 2011, Mike Gowanlock and Javad Lavaei(Gowanlock & Lavaei, 2011) conducted a study on
predicting the outcomes of MMA fights using a machine learning algorithm. The study used data on
various fighter attributes, such as age, weight, and height, to train the algorithm. Gowanlock and
Lavaei found that the machine learning algorithm was able to accurately predict the outcomes of
MMA fights.

A more recent study conducted in 2019 by Jean-Francois St-Onge and Mathieu Ouellette(St-Onge &
Ouellette, 2019) examined the use of machine learning algorithms to predict the outcomes of MMA
fights. The study used data on various fighter attributes, such as age, weight, and height, as well as
performance metrics, such as significant strikes and takedowns, to train the machine learning
algorithms. St-Onge and Ouellette found that machine learning algorithms were able to accurately
predict the outcomes of MMA fights.

Another study conducted in 2019 by Patrick Nolan and John W. Beal(Nolan & Beal, 2019) examined
the factors that influence the outcomes of MMA fights. The study analyzed data on various fighter
attributes, such as age, weight, and height, as well as performance metrics, such as significant strikes
and takedowns. Nolan and Beal found that significant strikes and takedowns were the most significant
predictors of fight outcomes.

In conclusion, there have been several studies on statistical analysis in MMA, which have examined
various factors that influence the outcomes of MMA fights. These studies have used various statistical
techniques, such as machine learning algorithms and multivariate regression, to analyze data on fighter
attributes and performance metrics. The results of these studies have shown that physical
characteristics such as height and reach are not significant predictors of fight outcomes, and that
factors such as age, experience, significant strikes, and takedowns are more significant predictors of
fight outcomes.

2.2 Review of Regression Analysis and Applications in Sports

Regression analysis is a statistical method that is widely used in sports to examine the relationship
between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables. In the field of sports,
regression analysis is useful in identifying the factors that contribute to success and predicting
outcomes (Wilson, 2013; James et al., 2014).

The application of regression analysis in sports is primarily focused on player evaluation. Teams use
data on player performance to identify the factors that contribute to success and use this information to
inform recruitment and trading decisions. The key metrics that predict success for a particular position,
such as goals scored for a forward in soccer or yards gained for a running back in football, can be
determined through regression analysis (Albert and Koning, 2008).

Regression analysis is also used in evaluating team performance by examining data on team
performance and identifying the factors that contribute to success. Coaches can use this information to
make informed decisions about player recruitment and trading, as well as adjust their strategies. For
instance, regression analysis can be used to determine the key metrics that predict success for a
particular game, such as the number of successful tackles for a defense in football (Carling et al.,
2005).

Another application of regression analysis in sports is in predicting outcomes. By examining data on
past performance and identifying the factors that contribute to success, analysts can make informed
predictions about the outcome of future events. Analysts may use regression analysis to predict the
likelihood of a team winning a game or a player performing at a certain level (Baumer et al., 2017).

Different types of regression analysis can be used in sports, including linear regression, logistic
regression, and Poisson regression. Linear regression is used to examine the relationship between a
dependent variable and one or more independent variables. Logistic regression is used to examine the
relationship between a dependent variable and a set of predictor variables that may be categorical or
continuous (Agresti, 2013).

Regression analysis has proven to be a valuable tool in sports, providing insights into player and team
performance and informing decision-making. However, it is important to recognize the limitations and
challenges of regression analysis. The quality of the data used in regression analysis is crucial, and
there is always the risk of overfitting the model to the data. Additionally, the assumptions underlying
regression analysis must be carefully considered to ensure that the results are valid (James et al.,
2013).

4.3 Concordant and discordant pairs

In binary classification problems, such as predicting the outcome of an MMA fight, concordant and
discordant pairs are used to evaluate the performance of predictive models in statistical analysis. A
concordant pair refers to two observations where the predicted outcome and the actual outcome agree.
On the other hand, a discordant pair refers to two observations where the predicted outcome and the
actual outcome disagree. For example, if a predictive model predicts that fighter A will win a fight,
and fighter A wins, this is a concordant pair. Conversely, if the predictive model predicts that fighter A
will win, but fighter B wins, this is a discordant pair.

The percentage of concordant pairs can serve as a measure of a model's predictive power. A higher
percentage of concordant pairs indicates that the model is better at correctly predicting outcomes.
However, discordant pairs should also be taken into consideration as they reflect cases where the
model fails to accurately predict the outcome.

In the context of predicting MMA fight outcomes, you can utilize the percentage of concordant and
discordant pairs to assess the performance of your predictive model. By computing these percentages
for different models, you can compare their predictive power and select the one with the highest
percentage of concordant pairs while minimizing the number of discordant pairs.

4.4 Rescaled R-Squared

In our report on predicting outcomes of MMA fights, we will use the rescaled R-squared statistic as a
measure of the goodness of fit of our predictive models. The rescaled R-squared is a modified version
of the standard R-squared that adjusts for the number of variables in the model. It ranges from 0 to 1,
where a value closer to 1 indicates a better fit of the model to the data (Mittlb├Čck and Schemper, 1996;
Kv├źlseth, 1985).

We will use the rescaled R-squared to compare the performance of different predictive models in our
analysis. By calculating the rescaled R-squared for each model, we can evaluate how well the model
fits the data and how well it predicts the outcomes of the MMA fights. A higher rescaled R-squared
value indicates a better fit of the model to the data and a higher predictive power of the model.
Furthermore, we will use the rescaled R-squared in conjunction with other statistical measures, such as
the Brier score, to assess the overall performance of our predictive models. The combination of
different statistical measures allows us to evaluate different aspects of the models' performance and
choose the model that provides the most accurate predictions of MMA fight outcomes. Overall, the use
of the rescaled R-squared statistic in our report will enable us to assess the quality of our predictive
models and provide valuable insights into the factors that influence the outcomes of MMA fights.

4.5 Cross-validation

The cross-validation techniques are essential for ensuring the statistical soundness of the final model
for predicting the outcomes of MMA fights. Cross-validation is a process where the model is tested on
a different set of data than the one used to train it. This technique helps to determine if the model is
useful and reliable for predicting the outcomes of MMA fights (Kohavi, 1995; James et al., 2013). By
dividing the data into training and testing sets, we can evaluate the performance of the model in
predicting outcomes and make necessary adjustments to improve its accuracy.

It is important to note that discriminant analysis has some limitations that restrict its applicability.
These limitations will be discussed in the Limitations section of the report. However, despite its
limitations, it remains a valuable tool in creating a predictive model for MMA fight outcomes
(Lachenbruch and Goldstein, 1979).

6. Conclusion and limitations

The aim of this study was to assess the effectiveness of novel variables, created from first level
variables, in predicting the outcomes of mixed martial arts (MMA) fights. These newly created
variables were chosen based on their relevance to measuring different aspects of MMA skills, and their
interpretability to those familiar with the sport. Using logistic regression analysis, the data from the
dataset was used to build models that could forecast the win/loss outcomes of MMA bouts and
compared to various baseline measures such as random chance, winning percentage, and first-level
variables.

The logistic regression models based on the novel second-level variables were significantly superior to
random chance and to a model using winning percentage to forecast outcomes. However, the first level
'count' variables were found to be significantly more predictive than the second-level variables alone.
A combination of both first and second-level variables produced the best model overall.

                         22
Cross-validation of the final model showed reasonable performance when predicting previously
unknown data. While the second-level variables were found to be inferior to the first level 'count'
variables when considered separately, their inclusion in the combined model improved the predictive
power and accuracy compared to earlier models. Overall, the project successfully created several
significant second-level variables that can forecast the win/loss results of MMA fights and provided
insights into how these variables can be used alongside traditional 'count' variables in predicting
outcomes.

Abstract

The main objective of this research is to develop a predictive model for determining the outcomes of
mixed martial arts (MMA) fights using statistical analysis. The model incorporates both traditional
first level variable and newer, constructed variables that offer a unique perspective on the sport.
Logistic regression is employed to predict wins and losses based on these variables, and the model's
predictive ability is then compared to that of baseline models. To achieve this goal, data on MMA
fights is collected and preprocessed using statistical software, including SPSS. Various statistical
techniques are utilized for feature engineering and selection to identify the most important variables
that impact the outcome of fights. The chosen variables are then used to develop the logistic regression
model, which is fine-tuned to optimize its predictive ability. The model is found to have a significantly
higher predictive ability than the baseline models, as confirmed by cross-validation. The constructed
variables provide a unique perspective on the sport and offer insights into the factors that contribute to
success in MMA.

1 Introduction

In recent years, statistical analysis in sports has become an important area of research for both
academics and professionals. Statistical methods are widely used in various sports to improve
performance, strategy, and player evaluation. For instance, in baseball, Sabermetrics involves detailed
statistical analysis to measure player performance and make strategic decisions (Lewis, 2003). In
basketball, advanced metrics such as Player Efficiency Rating (PER) and Win Shares help in assessing
a player's overall contribution to the team (Hollinger, 2002). Similarly, in soccer, data analysis tools
like Expected Goals (xG) and player tracking systems are utilized to evaluate team performance and
tactics (Anderson and Sally, 2013).

However, mixed martial arts (MMA) have received little attention in terms of statistical analysis.
While MMA has grown in popularity over the years, especially with the establishment of regulated
leagues like the Ultimate Fighting Championship (UFC), the use of advanced statistical methods in
this sport is still in its nascent stages. This presents an opportunity for further research and application
of statistical techniques to enhance understanding and performance in MMA.

Despite the tremendous growth of MMA as a sport and the large fan base it has garnered, there is a
shortage of statistical models to predict the outcomes of fights. This lack of statistical analysis has left
a gap in the ability to understand matchups between fighters and predict winners. Predicting outcomes
is not only valuable for analyzing the sport but is also of significant interest for betting purposes.
Therefore, this study aims to develop novel statistical models to predict the outcomes of MMA fights.
We will develop models based on new statistical methods and compare them with more basic models
that rely on winning percentages.

To achieve this objective, we will collect and preprocess data on MMA fights, and then engage in
feature engineering and selection to identify the most relevant variables. We will use regression
models with carefully chosen parameters to predict the outcomes of fights and evaluate the
performance of these models using metrics such as the Brier score and rescaled R-squared (Barnett,
2019). The models will be validated using cross-validation. The overall analysis process will involve a
careful selection of statistical techniques to ensure the models are statistically sound.

1.1 Background and Context of MMA as a Sport

Mixed Martial Arts (MMA) is a dynamic full-contact combat sport that has rapidly gained popularity
over the past few decades, with millions of fans tuning in to watch events such as the Ultimate
Fighting Championship (UFC). MMA allows the use of both striking and grappling techniques, both
standing or on the ground, from a wide range of martial arts and combat sports. The origins of MMA
can be traced back to the early 1900s when various forms of full-contact fighting were practiced in
different parts of the world. However, it was the creation of the UFC in 1993 that brought MMA into

                                                             2
the mainstream in North America. During the early days of the UFC, there were no rules or
regulations, and fights descended into brutal brawls, drawing criticism from politicians and the media
(Hoffman, 2020). To make the sport safer and more appealing to a wider audience, the UFC
implemented a set of rules in 2000. Today, MMA is a highly regulated sport, and strict rules govern
everything from the size of the fighting area to the types of strikes that are allowed. Fighters compete
in different weight classes, and they are subjected to rigorous medical testing before and after fights to
ensure their safety. Although MMA has faced criticism for its violent nature, proponents of the sport
argue that it is no more dangerous than other combat sports, such as boxing, and that the strict
regulations in place make it as safe as possible for athletes.

MMA has also been credited with breaking down barriers between different martial arts styles, as
fighters from various backgrounds compete against each other. This has led to the development of new
training methods and techniques as fighters seek to gain an edge over their opponents. Overall, MMA
is a sport that continues to evolve and grow, with millions of fans around the world tuning in to watch
the action (Mendelson, 2016). While it has faced criticism in the past, its strict regulations and
growing popularity suggest that it will continue to be a major force in the world of combat sports for
years to come (Dyer et al., 2016).

1.2 Rules and Judging

MMA judging is a process of evaluating and scoring fights to determine the winner. The scoring
system used is called the 10-point must system, where each round is scored separately by three judges.
In the UFC, fights are typically divided into three rounds for non-title fights and five rounds for
championship bouts. Each round is assessed based on four key factors: effective striking, grappling,
octagon control, and effective aggression (Weber and Fromm, 2012).

    ŌĆó Effective striking refers to clean and impactful strikes that land on the opponent, such as
         punches, kicks, knees, and elbows. Judges consider the quality, quantity, impact, and accuracy
         of these strikes when scoring.

    ŌĆó Grappling involves techniques like takedowns, submission attempts, and ground control.
         Judges evaluate how effective a fighter's grappling techniques are, including their ability to
         execute takedowns, reverse positions, and attempt submissions. Maintaining control on the
         ground and advancing position can also earn points.

    ŌĆó Octagon control is about assessing which fighter is dictating the pace and location of the fight.
         The fighter who controls the center of the octagon and is more active in engaging their
         opponent usually receives points in this category.

    ŌĆó Effective aggression refers to a fighter who consistently moves forward, presses the action,

2.2 Review of Regression Analysis and Applications in Sports

Regression analysis is a statistical method that is widely used in sports to examine the relationship
between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables. In the field of sports,
regression analysis is useful in identifying the factors that contribute to success and predicting
outcomes (Wilson, 2013; James et al., 2014).

The application of regression analysis in sports is primarily focused on player evaluation. Teams use
data on player performance to identify the factors that contribute to success and use this information to
inform recruitment and trading decisions. The key metrics that predict success for a particular position,
such as goals scored for a forward in soccer or yards gained for a running back in football, can be
determined through regression analysis (Albert and Koning, 2008).

Regression analysis is also used in evaluating team performance by examining data on team
performance and identifying the factors that contribute to success. Coaches can use this information to
make informed decisions about player recruitment and trading, as well as adjust their strategies. For
instance, regression analysis can be used to determine the key metrics that predict success for a
particular game, such as the number of successful tackles for a defense in football (Carling et al.,
2005).

Another application of regression analysis in sports is in predicting outcomes. By examining data on
past performance and identifying the factors that contribute to success, analysts can make informed
predictions about the outcome of future events. Analysts may use regression analysis to predict the
likelihood of a team winning a game or a player performing at a certain level (Baumer et al., 2017).

Different types of regression analysis can be used in sports, including linear regression, logistic
regression, and Poisson regression. Linear regression is used to examine the relationship between a
dependent variable and one or more independent variables. Logistic regression is used to examine the
relationship between a dependent variable and a set of predictor variables that may be categorical or
continuous (Agresti, 2013).

Regression analysis has proven to be a valuable tool in sports, providing insights into player and team
performance and informing decision-making. However, it is important to recognize the limitations and
challenges of regression analysis. The quality of the data used in regression analysis is crucial, and
there is always the risk of overfitting the model to the data. Additionally, the assumptions underlying
regression analysis must be carefully considered to ensure that the results are valid (James et al.,
2013).

4.3 Concordant and discordant pairs

In binary classification problems, such as predicting the outcome of an MMA fight, concordant and
discordant pairs are used to evaluate the performance of predictive models in statistical analysis. A
concordant pair refers to two observations where the predicted outcome and the actual outcome agree.
On the other hand, a discordant pair refers to two observations where the predicted outcome and the
actual outcome disagree. For example, if a predictive model predicts that fighter A will win a fight,
and fighter A wins, this is a concordant pair. Conversely, if the predictive model predicts that fighter A
will win, but fighter B wins, this is a discordant pair.

The percentage of concordant pairs can serve as a measure of a model's predictive power. A higher
percentage of concordant pairs indicates that the model is better at correctly predicting outcomes.
However, discordant pairs should also be taken into consideration as they reflect cases where the
model fails to accurately predict the outcome.

In the context of predicting MMA fight outcomes, you can utilize the percentage of concordant and
discordant pairs to assess the performance of your predictive model. By computing these percentages
for different models, you can compare their predictive power and select the one with the highest
percentage of concordant pairs while minimizing the number of discordant pairs.

In our report on predicting outcomes of MMA fights, we will use the rescaled R-squared statistic as a
measure of the goodness of fit of our predictive models. The rescaled R-squared is a modified version
of the standard R-squared that adjusts for the number of variables in the model. It ranges from 0 to 1,
where a value closer to 1 indicates a better fit of the model to the data (Mittlb├Čck and Schemper, 1996;
Kv├źlseth, 1985).

We will use the rescaled R-squared to compare the performance of different predictive models in our
analysis. By calculating the rescaled R-squared for each model, we can evaluate how well the model
fits the data and how well it predicts the outcomes of the MMA fights. A higher rescaled R-squared
value indicates a better fit of the model to the data and a higher predictive power of the model.
Furthermore, we will use the rescaled R-squared in conjunction with other statistical measures, such as
the Brier score, to assess the overall performance of our predictive models. The combination of
different statistical measures allows us to evaluate different aspects of the models' performance and
choose the model that provides the most accurate predictions of MMA fight outcomes. Overall, the use

estimates of Log (Punch Ratio) and Takedown efficiency were both positive, indicating that as values
for difference in Log(Punch Ratio) and Takedown efficiency increased, the log odds of winning also

                                                             15
increased, which is intuitively plausible. However, Punch Ratio had a negative estimate, which at first
seemed counterintuitive until we realized that Punch Ratio and Log (Punch Ratio) measured the same
aspect of the fight, one on a transformed log scale.

Further examination of Punch Ratio showed that the equation -0.0496+ 0.483 * ln(x) increased from 0
to approximately 9.7, meaning that as the difference in Punch Ratio increased from 0 to 9.7, the log
odds of winning increased. This range covers most of all fights. These results were somewhat
surprising because the univariate model based on the log (Punch Ratio) variable outperformed the
multiple logistic regression model including all variables in terms of percent concordance, rescaled R-
squared, and Brier score.

Even after reducing the model to only include variables that were significant at the 0.01 level, the
univariate model based on log (Punch Ratio) still performed better than the more complex model that
included Punch Ratio, log (Punch Ratio), and Takedown efficiency. This leaves us with a difficult
choice between the simpler univariate model and the more complex model. Choosing the simpler
model would discard two significant variables, while choosing the latter model would add two
variables that slightly decrease the predictive power of the model.

However, given that log (Punch Ratio) is highly correlated with both Punch Ratio and Takedown
effiency, we opt to utilize the univariate model with only log (Punch Ratio) and discard the other
variables.

6. Conclusion and limitations

The aim of this study was to assess the effectiveness of novel variables, created from first level
variables, in predicting the outcomes of mixed martial arts (MMA) fights. These newly created
variables were chosen based on their relevance to measuring different aspects of MMA skills, and their
interpretability to those familiar with the sport. Using logistic regression analysis, the data from the
dataset was used to build models that could forecast the win/loss outcomes of MMA bouts and
compared to various baseline measures such as random chance, winning percentage, and first-level
variables.

The logistic regression models based on the novel second-level variables were significantly superior to
random chance and to a model using winning percentage to forecast outcomes. However, the first level
'count' variables were found to be significantly more predictive than the second-level variables alone.
A combination of both first and second-level variables produced the best model overall.

                         21
Cross-validation of the final model showed reasonable performance when predicting previously
unknown data. While the second-level variables were found to be inferior to the first level 'count'
variables when considered separately, their inclusion in the combined model improved the predictive
power and accuracy compared to earlier models. Overall, the project successfully created several
significant second-level variables that can forecast the win/loss results of MMA fights and provided
insights into how these variables can be used alongside traditional 'count' variables in predicting
outcomes.
